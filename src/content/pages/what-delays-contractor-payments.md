## Approval and inspection processes

Payment doesn’t happen the day you submit. [Contractor draw schedules](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) and [progress billing](/contractor-progress-billing-cash-flow) require **approval**: the owner, GC, or lender reviews the application, may perform an inspection, and then approves for payment. Each step [delays contractor payments](/what-delays-contractor-payments). Large or commercial jobs often have lender-controlled disbursement, which can add more review time. There’s no way to eliminate this entirely, but you can **plan for it** and **fund the wait** with [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) when you have submitted but unpaid applications. For more on the draw process, see [contractor draw schedule cash flow](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow). For what to do when invoices are delayed, see [what contractors do when invoices are delayed](/what-contractors-do-when-invoices-delayed).

## Contract payment terms

[Construction payment terms](/construction-payment-terms-explained) are set in the contract. Net-30, net-60, net-90 mean payment is **due** 30, 60, or 90 days after invoice or approval—so the delay is built in. [Contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) often pay at the end of that window or later. When you bid, factor that timing into your [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) and pricing. Use [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) to cover the period between work and payment. [Accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) and [contractor invoice financing](/contractor-invoice-financing) can turn those receivables into immediate cash. For contract terms in depth, see [construction payment terms explained](/construction-payment-terms-explained) and [construction invoice payment delays](/construction-invoice-payment-delays).

## Retainage and back-loaded payment

[Retainage](/contractor-retainage-cash-flow) holds a percentage of payment until project completion. So part of your money is **delayed by design** until the job is done and accepted. That’s one of the structural [things that delay contractor payments](/what-delays-contractor-payments). You need to fund labor and materials through the job while that portion is held. [Contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) and [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) can bridge the gap. For retainage-specific planning, see [contractor retainage cash flow](/contractor-retainage-cash-flow).

## Payment runs and internal processes

Many owners and GCs pay on a **schedule**—e.g. once or twice a month. So even after approval, your payment may wait for the next “payment run.” That’s another source of [contractor payment delays](/what-delays-contractor-payments). You can’t change their process, but you can **anticipate** it and have [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) in place. When you have an approved invoice waiting for the run, [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) can advance against it so you get cash sooner. For [contractor waiting on invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices) and what to do, see [contractor waiting on invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices) and [what contractors do when invoices are delayed](/what-contractors-do-when-invoices-delayed).

## Disputes and change orders

When there’s a **dispute** (quantity, quality, scope) or a **change order** pending approval, payment on that portion can be delayed until it’s resolved. That can hold up a whole application or a line item. **Mitigation**: Keep documentation clear, resolve disputes quickly where possible, and don’t rely on disputed amounts for short-term cash flow. Use [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) to cover you during resolution. ## How to build delay into your cash flow forecast

Because [contractor payments get delayed](/what-delays-contractor-payments) for structural reasons—approval, terms, retainage, payment runs—you can **forecast** the delay rather than hope for the best. Assume each progress payment or invoice will take **at least** the contract term (e.g. net-60) plus 1–2 weeks for approval and processing. Build that into your [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) model so you know when you'll need [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit). When [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) are in the mix, add another buffer. The goal isn't to eliminate the delay (you often can't) but to **fund it** so [contractor cash flow problems](/contractor-cash-flow-problems) don't become crises.
