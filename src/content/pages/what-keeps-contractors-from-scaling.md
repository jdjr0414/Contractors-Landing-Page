## The cash gap that blocks growth

Scaling means spending more *before* you get paid: more [contractor payroll](/contractor-payroll-funding), more [contractor material purchase](/contractor-material-purchase-financing), more [contractor mobilization costs](/contractor-mobilization-costs). Payment still comes on [contractor draw schedules](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) or after [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) pay. That timing gap is one of the biggest things that **keeps contractors from scaling**—you literally can’t fund the next job or the next crew without [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) support. [Contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) and a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) are built for this: they bridge the period between spending and getting paid so you can take on more work without [contractor cash flow problems](/contractor-cash-flow-problems) stopping you. For why you keep hitting crunches, see [reasons your construction company keeps hitting cash crunches](/reasons-construction-company-cash-crunches).

## Bonding capacity limits

If you want to bid on public or large commercial work, you need [bonding](/contractor-bonding-and-financing). When sureties limit your capacity—or you can’t get bonded at all—you’re capped on the size and number of projects you can pursue. That’s a direct **scaling blocker**. Bonding is tied to your financial strength: working capital, liquidity, credit. [Contractor bonding and financing](/contractor-bonding-and-financing) go together: improving your balance sheet with [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) can help you qualify for larger bonds. For why contractors can’t secure bonding, see [why contractors can't secure bonding](/why-contractors-cant-secure-bonding); for losing work because of bonding, see [why contractors lose projects because of bonding](/why-contractors-lose-projects-bonding).

## Equipment and mobilization barriers

Taking on bigger or more jobs often means more or better equipment. If you can’t [afford equipment](/how-contractors-afford-heavy-equipment) or [equipment for new jobs](/how-contractors-afford-equipment-new-jobs), you’re stuck at your current capacity. [Construction equipment financing](/construction-equipment-financing)—for [excavators](/excavator-financing-contractors), [skid steers](/skid-steer-financing-contractors), [loaders](/loader-financing-contractors), [dump trucks](/dump-truck-financing-contractors)—lets you add machinery without draining the cash you need for [contractor payroll](/contractor-payroll-funding) and materials. Similarly, [contractor mobilization costs](/contractor-mobilization-costs) can be funded with [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) so you don’t hit [contractor cash flow between projects](/contractor-cash-flow-between-projects) before the first draw. For what stops contractors from buying equipment, see [what stops contractors from buying equipment](/what-stops-contractors-buying-equipment).

## Payroll and material timing

You have to pay crews and suppliers before [contractor invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices) are paid. When you scale, that burden grows. [Reasons contractors miss payroll](/reasons-contractors-miss-payroll) often tie to this exact gap: not enough liquidity between [progress billing](/contractor-progress-billing-cash-flow) or [contractor retainage](/contractor-retainage-cash-flow) and payday. [Contractor payroll funding](/contractor-payroll-funding) and [contractor material purchase financing](/contractor-material-purchase-financing) are designed for this. So is [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) when you have outstanding invoices—it turns [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) into immediate cash so you can cover payroll and materials. Without these tools, scaling means stretching cash to the breaking point. For more on payroll gaps, see [contractor payroll between jobs](/contractor-payroll-between-jobs).

## Lack of a funding strategy

Many contractors try to scale on cash flow alone. When [contractor cash flow between projects](/contractor-cash-flow-between-projects) or [seasonal contractor cash flow](/contractor-seasonal-cash-flow) dips, growth stalls or reverses. Having a **funding strategy**—a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) for recurring gaps, [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) for short-term needs, [construction equipment financing](/construction-equipment-financing) for equipment—lets you commit to more work without betting the company. ## When growth outpaces your current funding

Scaling only works if your **funding grows with your workload**. If you're still operating on the same [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) limit you had two years ago, but you're bidding and winning more or larger jobs, you'll hit the ceiling. [Why contractors max out their line of credit](/why-contractors-max-out-line-of-credit) is often a scaling story: the business grew, the need for liquidity grew, but the facility didn't. Revisit your [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) or [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) periodically and request increases as revenue and backlog grow. [Construction expansion funding](/construction-expansion-funding) and [construction business loans](/construction-business-loans) can provide a larger capital base when you're deliberately stepping up. That way [what keeps contractors from scaling](/what-keeps-contractors-from-scaling) becomes less about funding and more about execution. If you're already [maxing out your line of credit](/why-contractors-max-out-line-of-credit) or hitting [recurring cash crunches](/reasons-construction-company-cash-crunches), that's a sign your liquidity hasn't kept pace with growth—address that before taking on even more work. [Construction expansion funding](/construction-expansion-funding) is designed for contractors who are deliberately scaling; it can provide a larger capital base so [what keeps contractors from scaling](/what-keeps-contractors-from-scaling) is no longer funding-related. [Contractor bonding and financing](/contractor-bonding-and-financing) and [construction equipment financing](/construction-equipment-financing) address bonding and equipment barriers; [contractor payroll funding](/contractor-payroll-funding) and [contractor material purchase financing](/contractor-material-purchase-financing) address labor and materials so you can take on more work without hitting a cash wall. [What keeps contractors from scaling](/what-keeps-contractors-from-scaling) is usually one or more of these; fix each with the right funding and the ceiling lifts.
