## The gap is bigger than your line

A [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) is meant to bridge the period between spending (payroll, materials, [mobilization](/contractor-mobilization-costs)) and getting paid ([draw schedule](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow), [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients)). When that gap is **larger or longer** than your line, you [max out](/why-contractors-max-out-line-of-credit). More jobs, bigger jobs, or longer [construction invoice payment delays](/construction-invoice-payment-delays) all increase how much you need at once. So [reasons your construction company keeps hitting cash crunches](/reasons-construction-company-cash-crunches) and [why contractors max out their line of credit](/why-contractors-max-out-line-of-credit) are related: the need for liquidity outstrips the facility. **Options**: Request a line increase if your financials support it; add [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) so you’re not relying on the LOC alone. For qualification barriers, see [what stops contractors from qualifying for a line of credit](/what-stops-contractors-qualifying-line-of-credit).

## Using the line for everything

If you use the [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) for **operations and** for one-off needs—e.g. [construction equipment](/construction-equipment-financing), a large material buy, or a tax payment—you burn through the limit fast. The LOC works best for **revolving** operational gaps. **Equipment** should be funded with [construction equipment financing](/construction-equipment-financing) so you preserve LOC capacity for [contractor payroll](/contractor-payroll-funding) and [contractor material purchase](/contractor-material-purchase-financing). For the distinction, see [working capital vs equipment financing](/working-capital-vs-equipment-financing-contractors) and [job financing vs line of credit](/contractor-job-financing-vs-line-of-credit).

## Payment delays keep the line drawn longer

When [contractor invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices) and [payment applications](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) are delayed, you stay drawn on the line longer than you planned. That can push you to the limit and leave no buffer for the next gap. **Fix**: [Accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) and [contractor invoice financing](/contractor-invoice-financing) convert outstanding invoices to cash. You can use that to **pay down the line** and restore capacity, or to fund operations without drawing the line as deep. For [what delays contractor payments](/what-delays-contractor-payments) and [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients), see those guides.

## Seasonal or project spikes

[Contractor seasonal cash flow](/contractor-seasonal-cash-flow) or a few big jobs can create **spikes** in how much you need at once. If your line was sized for “average” gaps, you’ll [max out](/why-contractors-max-out-line-of-credit) in peak periods. **Mitigation**: Plan for the high months; consider [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) increase before the spike. [Accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) can help when you have a batch of invoices to convert. For seasonal planning, see [contractor seasonal cash flow](/contractor-seasonal-cash-flow).

## What to do when you're at the limit

**Short term**: Use [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) to turn [contractor waiting on invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices) into cash and pay down the line. **Medium term**: Fund [equipment](/construction-equipment-financing) with equipment financing so the LOC isn’t used for machinery. **Longer term**: Request a higher line or add [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) so your total liquidity matches your [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) needs. ## When to ask for a line increase

If you're consistently [maxing out](/why-contractors-max-out-line-of-credit) your [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit), it may be time to **request an increase**. Lenders are more likely to approve when you can show **higher revenue**, **strong repayment history**, and **clear use** for the additional capacity. Bring updated financials, a short explanation of how you use the line (e.g. [contractor payroll](/contractor-payroll-funding), [contractor material purchase](/contractor-material-purchase-financing), [contractor draw schedule](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) gaps), and evidence that you've outgrown the current limit. If your lender won't increase, consider **adding** [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) so your total liquidity matches your [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) needs. For [what stops contractors from qualifying for a line of credit](/what-stops-contractors-qualifying-line-of-credit), see that guide if you're applying for a first line. If you're at the limit because [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) or [contractor draw schedule](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) delays keep you drawn for longer than expected, [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) can convert those receivables to cash and help you pay down the line, restoring capacity for the next gap. [Why contractors max out their line of credit](/why-contractors-max-out-line-of-credit) is often a mix of timing and facility size; addressing both—faster conversion of receivables and a higher or additional facility—stops the squeeze. [Contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) can supplement the line for one-off or seasonal spikes so you don't rely on a single facility; [job financing vs line of credit](/contractor-job-financing-vs-line-of-credit) explains when to use which. [Why contractors max out their line of credit](/why-contractors-max-out-line-of-credit) is common when [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) gaps grow faster than your facility—address both to stay ahead. Use [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) to free up the line when [contractor waiting on invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices) is the bottleneck. [Why contractors max out their line of credit](/why-contractors-max-out-line-of-credit) is common when gaps grow; request an increase or add [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) so your liquidity matches your [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) needs.
