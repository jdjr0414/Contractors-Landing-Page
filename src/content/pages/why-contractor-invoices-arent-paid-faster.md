## Contract terms set the baseline

[Construction payment terms](/construction-payment-terms-explained) are in the contract before you start. Net-30, net-60, net-90 mean the client has **30, 60, or 90 days** from invoice (or approval) to pay. So [contractor invoices aren't paid faster](/why-contractor-invoices-arent-paid-faster) in part because the contract **allows** that much time. With [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) and government or large commercial jobs, terms are often net-60 or net-90. You can’t get paid “faster” than the contract allows without renegotiating—and on many jobs you can’t renegotiate. What you can do is **plan** for the delay and **fund** the gap with [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors). For how payment terms work, see [construction payment terms explained](/construction-payment-terms-explained).

## Payment application and approval

On project work, payment usually follows a [payment application](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) or [progress billing](/contractor-progress-billing-cash-flow) process. You submit; the owner or GC reviews; there may be an inspection; then they approve. Only then does the “payment clock” (and often the net-term clock) start. So [what delays contractor payments](/what-delays-contractor-payments) also keeps [contractor invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices) from being paid faster. **You can**: submit applications correctly and on time, respond quickly to questions, and keep documentation clean so approval isn’t held up by errors. For the rest, use [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) or [contractor invoice financing](/contractor-invoice-financing) to get cash on submitted or approved invoices before the client’s payment run. For what to do when invoices are delayed, see [what contractors do when invoices are delayed](/what-contractors-do-when-invoices-delayed).

## Retainage holds a portion back

[Retainage](/contractor-retainage-cash-flow) means a percentage of each payment is held until the project is complete. So even “current” progress payments are **not** paid in full—part is delayed by design. That’s why [contractor invoices aren’t paid faster](/why-contractor-invoices-arent-paid-faster) on the retained amount; you only get it at the end. Plan for that in [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) and use [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) to cover the gap. For retainage in depth, see [contractor retainage cash flow](/contractor-retainage-cash-flow).

## Client payment runs and internal process

Many clients pay on a **schedule**—e.g. once or twice a month. So even after approval, your invoice may sit until the next run. That’s another reason [contractor invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices) aren’t paid faster. You can’t change their process, but you can **anticipate** it. [Accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) advances against approved or submitted invoices so you get cash without waiting for their run. [Contractor invoice financing](/contractor-invoice-financing) does the same. For [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) and how to work with them, see [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients).

## What you can control vs. what you fund

**Control**: Submit accurate, complete [payment applications](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) and invoices on time. Fix disputes and change-order documentation quickly so they don’t hold up approval. Negotiate shorter payment terms where you have leverage. **Fund**: When terms and client process mean you’ll wait, use [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital), a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit), or [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) so [contractor cash flow problems](/contractor-cash-flow-problems) don’t stall you. ## When receivables financing makes the most sense

[Contractor invoices aren't paid faster](/why-contractor-invoices-arent-paid-faster) often because of contract terms and client process—things you can't unilaterally change. [Accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) and [contractor invoice financing](/contractor-invoice-financing) are designed for that reality: you get cash on your [contractor waiting on invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices) or submitted payment applications so you don't have to wait for the client's run. It makes the most sense when you have **creditworthy clients** and **clear, approved or submittable invoices**. If your [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) are solid but slow, receivables financing turns that delay into immediate [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital). Combine it with a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) for gaps when you don't have specific invoices to advance against. For [what delays contractor payments](/what-delays-contractor-payments) and [what contractors do when invoices are delayed](/what-contractors-do-when-invoices-delayed), see those guides. Improving **invoicing speed and accuracy** also helps: the faster you submit correct [payment applications](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow), the sooner the approval clock starts, so even if [contractor invoices aren't paid faster](/why-contractor-invoices-arent-paid-faster) in absolute terms, you can shorten the wait by controlling your part of the process. [Contractor retainage](/contractor-retainage-cash-flow) and [construction payment terms](/construction-payment-terms-explained) set the outer limit on how fast you get paid; within that, [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) and [contractor invoice financing](/contractor-invoice-financing) let you get cash without waiting for the client. [Contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) and [what delays contractor payments](/what-delays-contractor-payments) go deeper on why payment is slow; the takeaway is that [why contractor invoices aren't paid faster](/why-contractor-invoices-arent-paid-faster) is structural, so financing the wait is the lever you control. [Accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) and [contractor invoice financing](/contractor-invoice-financing) turn slow invoices into immediate cash. Use them when [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) and contract terms mean you'll wait—so [why contractor invoices aren't paid faster](/why-contractor-invoices-arent-paid-faster) doesn't stall your operations.
