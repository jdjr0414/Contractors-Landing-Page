## What causes contractor cash flow problems?

The main cause is timing—expenses hit before revenue arrives. Payroll, materials, equipment, and overhead must be paid on schedule, while client payments often arrive weeks or months later. Construction businesses operate in a world where they spend first and get paid later. That fundamental mismatch creates cash flow pressure even when the business is profitable.

## Why do contractors run out of cash between jobs?

Labor must be paid between jobs. Materials may be purchased before the next project starts. Overhead continues. Revenue from the last job may not have arrived. The gap between jobs creates cash flow pressure. Contractors who rely on a steady stream of projects can find themselves short when there is a pause or when payment cycles stretch. [Contractor payroll funding](/contractor-payroll-funding) and [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) are designed to bridge these gaps.

## What are the most common contractor cash flow problems?

- **Waiting on invoices** – Work is done, but payment may not arrive for 30, 60, or 90 days.
- **Payroll between jobs** – Labor must be paid when payday arrives, even if project draws are delayed.
- **Material purchases** – Suppliers require payment before or upon delivery; client funds arrive later.
- **Equipment costs** – Repairs or purchases can drain reserves.
- **Seasonal slowdowns** – Revenue dips but expenses continue.
- **Job mobilization** – Starting a new job requires upfront spending before revenue arrives.
- **Expansion** – Growth opportunities require capital before new revenue streams.

## When is it a timing problem vs a profitability problem?

Timing problems occur when jobs are profitable but cash arrives too slowly. Profitability problems occur when the business is losing money. Funding can help with timing; it cannot fix fundamental profitability issues. Before seeking funding, understand whether the issue is when cash arrives or whether the business model is sustainable.

## What funding options help contractor cash flow?

[Contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) helps cover payroll, materials, and short-term gaps. A [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) provides flexible access for recurring needs. [Contractor payroll funding](/contractor-payroll-funding) addresses payroll timing specifically. [Contractor material purchase financing](/contractor-material-purchase-financing) helps with material timing. [Accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) helps when invoices are outstanding. [Construction equipment financing](/construction-equipment-financing) addresses equipment needs. Matching the product to the problem improves the fit.

## How to address cash flow before it becomes critical

Improve collections, negotiate payment terms with clients, and build reserves when possible. Use funding as a tool for timing gaps rather than as a permanent crutch. Understand your payment cycle and plan for gaps. Having a [line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) in place before you need it can provide peace of mind.
