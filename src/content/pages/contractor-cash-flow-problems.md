## What causes contractor cash flow problems?

The main cause is timing—expenses hit before revenue arrives. Payroll, materials, equipment, and overhead must be paid on schedule, while client payments often arrive weeks or months later. Construction businesses operate in a world where they spend first and get paid later. That fundamental mismatch creates cash flow pressure even when the business is profitable. Net-60 and net-90 payment terms are common. Retainage stretches the wait. Draw schedules may not align with expense cycles. Understanding this pattern is the first step toward finding solutions. This guide explains the most common [contractor cash flow problems](/contractor-cash-flow-problems) and the funding options that can help.

## Why do contractors run out of cash between jobs?

Labor must be paid between jobs. Materials may be purchased before the next project starts. Overhead continues. Revenue from the last job may not have arrived. The gap between jobs creates cash flow pressure. Contractors who rely on a steady stream of projects can find themselves short when there is a pause or when payment cycles stretch. Even during busy seasons, overlapping jobs with staggered payment schedules can create gaps—cash goes out across several projects while payments arrive at different times. [Contractor payroll funding](/contractor-payroll-funding) and [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) are designed to bridge these gaps. For seasonal patterns, see [how contractors handle slow winter months](/blog/contractors-slow-winter-months).

## What are the most common contractor cash flow problems?

- **Waiting on invoices** – Work is done, but payment may not arrive for 30, 60, or 90 days. See [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) for options.
- **Payroll between jobs** – Labor must be paid when payday arrives, even if project draws are delayed. See [contractor payroll funding](/contractor-payroll-funding).
- **Material purchases** – Suppliers require payment before or upon delivery; client funds arrive later. See [contractor material purchase financing](/contractor-material-purchase-financing).
- **Equipment costs** – Repairs or purchases can drain reserves. See [construction equipment financing](/construction-equipment-financing).
- **Seasonal slowdowns** – Revenue dips but expenses continue. A [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) may help.
- **Job mobilization** – Starting a new job requires upfront spending before revenue arrives.
- **Expansion** – Growth opportunities require capital before new revenue streams. See [construction business loans](/construction-business-loans).

## When is it a timing problem vs a profitability problem?

Timing problems occur when jobs are profitable but cash arrives too slowly. Profitability problems occur when the business is losing money. Funding can help with timing; it cannot fix fundamental profitability issues. Before seeking funding, understand whether the issue is when cash arrives or whether the business model is sustainable. If jobs are profitable and the problem is payment cycles, [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital), a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit), or other timing-focused products may help. If the business is unprofitable, funding may only delay the underlying problem.

## What financing options do construction businesses use?

Contractors have several options depending on the specific problem. **[Contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital)** helps cover payroll, materials, and short-term gaps. A **[contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit)** provides flexible access for recurring needs. **[Contractor payroll funding](/contractor-payroll-funding)** addresses payroll timing specifically. **[Contractor material purchase financing](/contractor-material-purchase-financing)** helps with material timing. **[Accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors)** helps when invoices are outstanding. **[Construction equipment financing](/construction-equipment-financing)** addresses equipment needs. **[Construction business loans](/construction-business-loans)** fit larger, longer-term needs like expansion or acquisition. Matching the product to the problem improves the fit.

## When does each funding option make sense?

**Working capital** fits one-time, urgent needs—a single payroll gap, material order, or mobilization. **A line of credit** fits recurring gaps—payroll float, seasonal slowdowns, or overlapping jobs. **Payroll funding** fits when the primary issue is labor timing. **Material purchase financing** fits when the primary issue is supplier payment timing. **Accounts receivable financing** fits when you have clear invoices from creditworthy clients. **Equipment financing** fits when you need machinery or vehicles. **Business loans** fit expansion, acquisition, or larger capital needs. ## How to address cash flow before it becomes critical

Improve collections, negotiate payment terms with clients, and build reserves when possible. Use funding as a tool for timing gaps rather than as a permanent crutch. Understand your payment cycle and plan for gaps. Having a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) in place before you need it can provide peace of mind. When you know a slow season or overlapping jobs are coming, securing access in advance can reduce stress.
