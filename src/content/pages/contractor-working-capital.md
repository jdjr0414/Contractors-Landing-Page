## What is contractor working capital?

Contractor working capital refers to funding used to manage day-to-day operating costs such as payroll, materials, fuel, and jobsite expenses. Contractors often seek working capital when client payments are delayed but expenses continue. Unlike equipment financing, which is tied to a specific asset, working capital supports the general operations that keep a construction business running. It is designed for short-term cash flow gaps rather than long-term capital purchases.

## Why do contractors need working capital?

Construction payment cycles rarely align with expense cycles. Labor must be paid weekly or biweekly. Materials are often purchased before milestones. Fuel, insurance, and overhead continue regardless of when invoices are paid. Many contractors face situations where work is done but payment is 30, 60, or 90 days out. Working capital helps bridge that timing gap so the business can keep operating without stalling or missing payments.

## When do contractors use working capital?

Contractors commonly use working capital for payroll between draws, material purchases before a milestone, mobilization costs for a new job, emergency repairs, or marketing and expansion efforts. The key is that the need is short-term and tied to cash flow timing rather than long-term profitability. If jobs are profitable but cash arrives too slowly, working capital is often a fit.

## What lenders often look at

Lenders typically review revenue history, time in business, bank activity, average deposits, and the reason for funds. Some products may require less documentation than traditional bank loans. Qualification requirements vary by product type. Understanding what you need and what you can provide helps narrow the options.

## Contractor working capital vs line of credit

Working capital can be faster and simpler for a one-time immediate need. A line of credit may offer more flexibility over time—you draw when needed and repay as cash comes in. The right fit depends on urgency, qualification, and whether you expect recurring gaps. For more on flexible access, see our guide on [contractor lines of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit).

## Contractor working capital vs equipment financing

Working capital is for operating expenses. Equipment financing is for machinery, vehicles, and tools. If you need to buy excavators, skid steers, or trucks, [construction equipment financing](/construction-equipment-financing) is typically the better fit. If you need to cover payroll or materials, working capital is the right category.

## Common use cases

- **Payroll between draws** – Labor must be paid when payday arrives, even if the project draw hasn't.
- **Material purchases** – Suppliers often require payment before delivery, while client funds may arrive later.
- **Mobilization costs** – Starting a new job requires upfront spending before revenue arrives.
- **Emergency repairs** – Equipment or facility issues can create unexpected cash needs.
- **Marketing or expansion** – Growing the business may require capital before new revenue streams.

## How to choose the right product

Consider your timeline, how often you need funds, and what you can qualify for. If the need is one-time and urgent, a working capital product may fit. If you expect recurring gaps, a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) could be worth exploring. If the problem is payroll-specific, [contractor payroll funding](/contractor-payroll-funding) may address it directly. For material purchases, [contractor material purchase financing](/contractor-material-purchase-financing) is another option.
