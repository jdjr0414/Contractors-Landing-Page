## Payroll is due before you get paid

The core reason [contractors miss payroll](/reasons-contractors-miss-payroll) is **timing**. You owe your crew on a fixed schedule. Your clients pay on [contractor draw schedules](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow), [progress billing](/contractor-progress-billing-cash-flow), or net-60/net-90 terms. So cash is often **in the pipeline**—in [contractor waiting on invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices) or [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients)—but not in the bank on payday. That’s a [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) problem, not necessarily a profitability problem. [Contractor payroll funding](/contractor-payroll-funding) and [contractor payroll between jobs](/contractor-payroll-between-jobs) exist specifically to close that gap. So do [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) and [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit). For the big picture, see [contractor cash flow problems](/contractor-cash-flow-problems).

## Draw and progress payment delays

You complete work, submit a payment application, and wait. Approval, [construction invoice payment delays](/construction-invoice-payment-delays), and processing can push payment 30–45 days or more past the work. In the meantime, [contractor payroll](/contractor-payroll-funding) is due. When **multiple draws** are delayed or **one big client** is slow, you can run short even when the job is profitable. **Fix**: Fund the gap with [contractor payroll funding](/contractor-payroll-funding) or [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital). When you have submitted but unpaid applications or invoices, [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) or [contractor invoice financing](/contractor-invoice-financing) can turn them into immediate cash for payroll. For draw timing, see [contractor draw schedule cash flow](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow).

## Slow payers and long payment terms

[Contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) and net-60/net-90 terms mean you’re funding labor (and materials) for months before payment. That’s one of the main [reasons contractors miss payroll](/reasons-contractors-miss-payroll). You’re not broke—you’re **illiquid**. [Accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) and [contractor invoice financing](/contractor-invoice-financing) let you get paid on your invoices so you can pay your people. A [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) can also smooth recurring gaps when you know slow pay is built into the contract. For more on slow pay, see [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) and [construction payment terms](/construction-payment-terms-explained).

## Between jobs and seasonal dips

[Contractor payroll between jobs](/contractor-payroll-between-jobs) is a real challenge: you may want to keep key people on the payroll between projects, but there’s no current job funding them. [Contractor seasonal cash flow](/contractor-seasonal-cash-flow) can create the same squeeze. Without a buffer, you may **miss payroll** or lay people off. [Contractor payroll funding](/contractor-payroll-funding) and [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) can cover the between-job or seasonal gap so you don’t lose your crew. For seasonal issues, see [contractor seasonal cash flow](/contractor-seasonal-cash-flow).

## Retainage and back-loaded cash

[Retainage](/contractor-retainage-cash-flow) holds a portion of payment until the project is complete. So you’re paying 100% of labor while a slice of revenue is held back. That **back-loads** your cash and can contribute to payroll pressure near the end of a job or when retainage is large. Factor retainage into your [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) plan and use [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) to cover the gap. For retainage details, see [contractor retainage cash flow](/contractor-retainage-cash-flow).

## What to do so you don’t miss payroll

**Plan for the gap.** Assume payment will be later than you hope. **Line up funding before you need it**: [contractor payroll funding](/contractor-payroll-funding), [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital), or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit). When you have invoices or payment applications outstanding, [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) can convert them to cash.
