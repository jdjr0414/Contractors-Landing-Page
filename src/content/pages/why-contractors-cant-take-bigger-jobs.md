## Not enough cash to fund the job

Bigger jobs mean more [contractor mobilization costs](/contractor-mobilization-costs), more [contractor payroll](/contractor-payroll-funding), more [contractor material purchase](/contractor-material-purchase-financing)—all before the first [draw](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) or [progress payment](/contractor-progress-billing-cash-flow). If you don’t have [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit), you literally **can’t fund** the job. That’s one of the main reasons [contractors can’t take on bigger jobs](/why-contractors-cant-take-bigger-jobs). [Contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) gaps get worse as job size increases. [Contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) and [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) bridge the period between spending and payment so you can take the work. For mid-project cash risk, see [why contractors run out of cash mid-project](/why-contractors-run-out-of-cash-mid-project). For scaling in general, see [what keeps contractors from scaling](/what-keeps-contractors-from-scaling).

## Bonding capacity limits

Larger projects usually require larger **bonds**. If your [bonding capacity](/contractor-bonding-and-financing) is capped, you can’t bid on jobs above that cap. So [why contractors can’t take on bigger jobs](/why-contractors-cant-take-bigger-jobs) often comes down to [why contractors can’t secure bonding](/why-contractors-cant-secure-bonding) at the level they need. Sureties look at working capital, liquidity, and credit. **Fix**: [Contractor bonding and financing](/contractor-bonding-and-financing)—using [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) to strengthen your balance sheet—can help sureties increase your bond limit. For losing work over bonding, see [why contractors lose projects because of bonding](/why-contractors-lose-projects-bonding) and [why contractor bids get rejected](/why-contractor-bids-get-rejected).

## Equipment and capacity

Bigger or more complex work often needs **more or different equipment**. If you can’t [afford equipment](/how-contractors-afford-heavy-equipment) or [equipment for new jobs](/how-contractors-afford-equipment-new-jobs), you’re capped. [Construction equipment financing](/construction-equipment-financing)—for [excavators](/excavator-financing-contractors), [skid steers](/skid-steer-financing-contractors), [loaders](/loader-financing-contractors), [dump trucks](/dump-truck-financing-contractors)—lets you add machinery without draining the cash you need for [contractor payroll](/contractor-payroll-funding) and materials. So [what stops contractors from buying equipment](/what-stops-contractors-buying-equipment) is related to [why contractors can’t take bigger jobs](/why-contractors-cant-take-bigger-jobs); solving equipment funding can unlock job size. For equipment decisions, see [construction equipment financing](/construction-equipment-financing) and [working capital vs equipment financing](/working-capital-vs-equipment-financing-contractors).

## Fear of running out of cash mid-job

Some contractors **could** technically take a bigger job but don’t because they’ve been burned before—[running out of cash mid-project](/why-contractors-run-out-of-cash-mid-project) or [reasons your construction company keeps hitting cash crunches](/reasons-construction-company-cash-crunches). The solution is **reliable funding** so the gap between [contractor draw schedule](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) and [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) doesn’t sink you. [Contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital), [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit), and [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) give you a buffer. [Construction expansion funding](/construction-expansion-funding) and [construction business loans](/construction-business-loans) can support a deliberate move into larger work. ## Labor and crew capacity

Beyond cash and bonding, **labor** can be a barrier. Bigger jobs may require more crew or different skills. If you don't have the people—or the cash to pay them before the first [draw](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow)—you can't scale. [Contractor payroll funding](/contractor-payroll-funding) and [contractor payroll between jobs](/contractor-payroll-between-jobs) help you keep key people on the payroll between projects so you're ready when a larger job comes. [Contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) can fund the first few weeks of a big job until progress payments start. So [why contractors can't take on bigger jobs](/why-contractors-cant-take-bigger-jobs) isn't only about equipment and bonding; it's also about having the liquidity to staff up and pay [contractor payroll](/contractor-payroll-funding) while [contractor invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices) are pending. Addressing payroll timing with dedicated funding can remove that piece of the barrier. Bigger jobs also often mean **larger mobilization** and **more materials** before the first [draw](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow). [Contractor mobilization costs](/contractor-mobilization-costs) and [contractor material purchase financing](/contractor-material-purchase-financing) can be funded with [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) so you're not stretching cash at the very moment you need to show you can perform.
