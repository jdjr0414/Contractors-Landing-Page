## The payment chain: you're last

Payment typically flows **owner → GC → sub**. The GC gets paid on their [draw schedule](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) or [progress billing](/contractor-progress-billing-cash-flow), then pays subs. So [what stops subcontractors from getting paid on time](/what-stops-subcontractors-getting-paid-on-time) is partly that you’re **last in line**. Any delay in [what delays contractor payments](/what-delays-contractor-payments) to the GC becomes a delay to you. You can’t change the chain, but you can **fund the wait** with [subcontractor financing](/subcontractor-financing)—[contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital), [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors), or [contractor invoice financing](/contractor-invoice-financing)—so you get cash on your invoices before the GC pays. For how payment timing works, see [contractor draw schedule cash flow](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) and [construction payment terms explained](/construction-payment-terms-explained).

## Draw and approval delays

The GC submits a payment application; the owner or lender reviews it; there may be inspections; then the GC gets paid. Only **after** that can the GC pay you. So [construction invoice payment delays](/construction-invoice-payment-delays) and [contractor slow paying](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) at the GC level directly delay when [subcontractors get paid](/what-stops-subcontractors-getting-paid-on-time). [Subcontractor financing](/subcontractor-financing) and [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) let you get paid on your receivable (your invoice to the GC) without waiting for their payment run. For [contractor waiting on invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices) and what to do, see [what contractors do when invoices are delayed](/what-contractors-do-when-invoices-delayed).

## Retainage and pay-when-paid terms

[Retainage](/contractor-retainage-cash-flow) often applies to subs: a portion of your payment is held until the project is complete. So part of what you’re owed is **delayed by design**. Some contracts also have **pay-when-paid** or **pay-if-paid** clauses—you get paid when (or if) the GC gets paid. That ties your timing to theirs. [Contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) can fund you during the retainage period. [Subcontractor financing](/subcontractor-financing) can cover the gap when the GC is slow. For retainage, see [contractor retainage cash flow](/contractor-retainage-cash-flow).

## Contract terms and payment runs

Your contract with the GC may specify **net-30, net-60**, or payment within X days of the GC’s receipt of payment. So even after the GC is paid, they may have 30–60 days to pay you. That’s another [reason subcontractors don’t get paid on time](/what-stops-subcontractors-getting-paid-on-time). [Accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) and [contractor invoice financing](/contractor-invoice-financing) advance against your invoices so you don’t have to wait for the GC’s payment run. For terms in general, see [construction payment terms explained](/construction-payment-terms-explained).

## What subs can do

**Negotiate** shorter payment terms where you have leverage. **Factor the wait** into your pricing. **Use [subcontractor financing](/subcontractor-financing)** so you’re not dependent on the GC’s timing: [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital), [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit), and [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) or [contractor invoice financing](/contractor-invoice-financing) can turn your receivables into immediate cash for [contractor payroll](/contractor-payroll-funding) and materials.
