## What is contractor working capital?

Contractor working capital refers to funding used to manage day-to-day operating costs such as payroll, materials, fuel, and jobsite expenses. Contractors often seek working capital when client payments are delayed but expenses continue. Unlike equipment financing, which is tied to a specific asset, working capital supports the general operations that keep a construction business running between invoice and payment.

## Why do contractors run out of cash between jobs?

Construction payment cycles rarely align with expense cycles. Labor must be paid weekly or biweekly, materials are often purchased before milestones, and equipment costs continue regardless of when invoices are paid. The gap between spending and receiving creates cash flow pressure. Many contractors face this when they are between draws on a project, waiting on retainage, or dealing with slow-paying clients. The business may be profitable on paper, but timing creates real cash shortages.

## How do contractors finance heavy equipment?

Contractors typically use equipment financing or equipment loans to purchase excavators, skid steers, dump trucks, and other machinery. Payments are structured to match the revenue the equipment generates, preserving working capital for payroll and operations. Equipment financing can cover new purchases, used equipment, and sometimes major repairs or upgrades. Lenders often use the equipment itself as collateral, which can make qualification easier than unsecured options.

## What financial options exist for contractors?

Contractors have several funding options depending on their situation. Working capital products help cover payroll, materials, and short-term gaps. Equipment financing is designed for machinery and vehicles. Lines of credit provide flexible, revolving access for recurring needs. Business loans can support larger projects or expansion. Accounts receivable financing helps when invoices are outstanding but bills are due. SBA loans, commercial real estate loans, and bridge loans may fit specific situations like expansion or acquisition.

## When does a particular funding type make sense?

Working capital fits when the need is immediate and short-term—payroll between draws, material purchases before a milestone, or mobilization costs. Equipment financing makes sense when a machine is needed to take on or complete jobs. A line of credit suits contractors with recurring timing gaps who want to draw and repay as needed. Accounts receivable financing helps when slow-paying clients create cash flow pressure. The right option depends on urgency, qualification, and how the funds will be used.

## Why contractors look for funding

Construction businesses often need money before customers pay. Materials, labor, fuel, and equipment costs hit first. Funding can help bridge that timing gap. Whether it is payroll between jobs, a material deposit for an upcoming project, or an equipment repair that cannot wait, contractors frequently face situations where cash is needed before it arrives.

## Common contractor cash flow problems

Contractors frequently face financial situations that create pressure. Waiting on invoices is one of the most common—work is done, but payment may not arrive for 30, 60, or 90 days. Payroll between jobs creates another gap: labor must be paid even when project draws are delayed. Material purchases often require upfront payment before client funds arrive. Equipment purchases or repairs can drain reserves or force difficult choices. Seasonal slowdowns, job mobilization costs, and expansion opportunities all create moments when extra capital would help. Understanding these patterns helps contractors plan and choose the right funding when needed.
