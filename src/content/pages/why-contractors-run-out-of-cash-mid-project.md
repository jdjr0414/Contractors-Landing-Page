## The timing trap: spending before payment

Mid-project cash crunches happen because you **spend before you get paid**. You mobilize, buy materials, pay [contractor payroll](/contractor-payroll-funding), and pay subs. Then you submit a [payment application](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) and wait—for approval, for [construction invoice payment delays](/construction-invoice-payment-delays), for the check. That gap is why [contractors run out of cash mid-project](/why-contractors-run-out-of-cash-mid-project). It’s not always poor planning; it’s the structure of [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) in construction. [Contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) and a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) are designed to fund that gap so you don’t stall the job or [miss payroll](/reasons-contractors-miss-payroll). For the full picture of payment timing, see [contractor draw schedule cash flow](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) and [contractor progress billing cash flow](/contractor-progress-billing-cash-flow).

## Draw schedule and approval delays

[Draw schedules](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) and [progress billing](/contractor-progress-billing-cash-flow) mean you get paid in chunks, not daily. Between chunks you’re funding labor, materials, and overhead. When **approval or payment is delayed**—owner review, lender inspection, [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients)—your cash runs out before the next draw. There’s no way to eliminate the wait entirely, but you can **fund it**: [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital), [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit), or [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) when you have submitted but unpaid applications or [contractor waiting on invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices). For what to do when invoices are delayed, see [what contractors do when invoices are delayed](/what-contractors-do-when-invoices-delayed).

## Change orders and scope creep

Change orders increase scope and cost. Often you perform the work **before** the change order is approved and paid. That means more out-of-pocket spend with no immediate payment. If you’re already tight on [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide), change orders can push you into a mid-project crunch. **Mitigation**: Build a cushion with [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) so you can absorb change-order timing. Include change-order payment terms in your contracts where possible. For recurring crunches, see [reasons your construction company keeps hitting cash crunches](/reasons-construction-company-cash-crunches).

## Retainage and back-loaded payment

[Retainage](/contractor-retainage-cash-flow) holds a portion of payment until project completion. So you’re funding 100% of the work while a percentage (often 5–10%) is held back. That **back-loads** your cash flow: you need more liquidity during the job than the payment schedule suggests. Factor retainage into your [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) plan and ensure you have [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) to cover the gap. For retainage-specific issues, see [contractor retainage cash flow](/contractor-retainage-cash-flow).

## Overlapping jobs and thin reserves

When you run multiple jobs, each has its own [draw schedule](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) and [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients). If draws don’t line up, you can hit a period where **several jobs need funding** and none have just paid. That’s when mid-project cash runs out even when no single job is “over budget.” **Solution**: Treat your **total** cash need across jobs, not per job. A [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) or [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) gives you a buffer for these overlaps. [Contractor payroll funding](/contractor-payroll-funding) can cover labor when receivables are delayed. For a full list of options, see [all funding options](/all-funding-options). ## Planning so mid-project crunches don't repeat

Once you've experienced [running out of cash mid-project](/why-contractors-run-out-of-cash-mid-project), the goal is to avoid repeating it. **Map the job's cash flow** before you start: when will you need to pay [contractor payroll](/contractor-payroll-funding), [contractor material purchase](/contractor-material-purchase-financing), and subs, and when do you expect each [draw](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) or [progress payment](/contractor-progress-billing-cash-flow)? Identify the widest gap and ensure you have [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) to cover it. If [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) or approval delays are likely, build in a buffer. Many contractors keep a **reserve draw** on their line of credit specifically for mid-project gaps so they're never one delayed payment away from a crisis. For recurring crunches, see [reasons your construction company keeps hitting cash crunches](/reasons-construction-company-cash-crunches). Some contractors also use [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) to convert submitted or approved payment applications into immediate cash, which shortens the wait and reduces how much [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or line you need to carry through the job. [Contractor retainage](/contractor-retainage-cash-flow) means a slice of each payment is held until completion, so your mid-project cash need is higher than the payment schedule suggests—plan for that when sizing your buffer so [why contractors run out of cash mid-project](/why-contractors-run-out-of-cash-mid-project) doesn't become your story. [Contractor draw schedule cash flow](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) and [contractor progress billing cash flow](/contractor-progress-billing-cash-flow) explain how milestone payments work; [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) and [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) fund the gap between milestones. Planning for [why contractors run out of cash mid-project](/why-contractors-run-out-of-cash-mid-project) before the job starts is the best way to avoid it. Size your buffer for the worst-case delay, not the best. [Contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) and [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) are the standard tools for bridging [why contractors run out of cash mid-project](/why-contractors-run-out-of-cash-mid-project).
