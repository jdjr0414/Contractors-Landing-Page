## Payment timing is built into construction

You spend first: [contractor payroll](/contractor-payroll-funding), [contractor material purchase](/contractor-material-purchase-financing), [contractor mobilization costs](/contractor-mobilization-costs). You get paid later: [contractor draw schedule](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow), [progress billing](/contractor-progress-billing-cash-flow), [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients). That **timing** is why [your construction company keeps hitting cash crunches](/reasons-construction-company-cash-crunches). It’s not necessarily that you’re unprofitable—it’s that cash is tied up in [contractor waiting on invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices) and [what delays contractor payments](/what-delays-contractor-payments). Without a buffer, every delay creates a crunch. [Contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) and a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) are that buffer. For the full picture, see [contractor cash flow problems](/contractor-cash-flow-problems) and [contractor cash flow guide](/contractor-cash-flow-guide).

## No buffer when the gap widens

When you run with **no** [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit), any delay—a slow draw, a [contractor slow paying](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) client, a dispute—turns into a crunch. Recurring crunches mean the gap is **predictable** but unfunded. **Fix**: Build a buffer. A [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) lets you draw when the gap hits and repay when payment arrives. [Contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) can cover short-term needs. Once the buffer is in place, the same timing that used to cause [reasons construction company cash crunches](/reasons-construction-company-cash-crunches) becomes manageable. For when the LOC isn’t enough, see [why contractors max out their line of credit](/why-contractors-max-out-line-of-credit).

## Retainage and back-loaded payment

[Retainage](/contractor-retainage-cash-flow) holds a slice of payment until the project is done. So you’re funding 100% of the job while part of your revenue is delayed. That **back-loads** cash flow and can cause crunches near the end of a job or when retainage is large. Factor retainage into planning and use [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) to cover the period. For retainage details, see [contractor retainage cash flow](/contractor-retainage-cash-flow).

## Seasonal and between-project gaps

[Contractor seasonal cash flow](/contractor-seasonal-cash-flow) and [contractor cash flow between projects](/contractor-cash-flow-between-projects) create periods when revenue dips but obligations (payroll, equipment, overhead) continue. If you don’t plan for those periods, they become another [reason your construction company keeps hitting cash crunches](/reasons-construction-company-cash-crunches). [Contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital), [contractor payroll funding](/contractor-payroll-funding), and a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) can fund the seasonal or between-job gap. For seasonal planning, see [contractor seasonal cash flow](/contractor-seasonal-cash-flow).

## Overlapping jobs and payment misalignment

When you run multiple jobs, each has its own [draw](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) and payment cycle. If several jobs need funding at once and none have just paid, you hit a crunch even when no single job is over budget. **Solution**: Size your **total** liquidity (working capital + LOC) for the combined gap across jobs. [Accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) can convert [contractor waiting on invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices) to cash and smooth those overlaps. ## Breaking the crunch cycle for good

Recurring [cash crunches](/reasons-construction-company-cash-crunches) usually mean the business has **outgrown** its funding buffer. The fix isn't just one more [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) loan—it's establishing a **reliable** buffer that scales with you. A [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) that you draw and repay as [contractor draw schedule](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) and [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) dictate can become your default backstop. Add [accounts receivable financing](/accounts-receivable-financing-contractors) when you have [contractor waiting on invoices](/contractor-waiting-on-invoices) so you're not solely dependent on the LOC. Once the buffer is in place and sized correctly, the same [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) timing that used to cause crunches becomes manageable. For [why contractors run out of cash mid-project](/why-contractors-run-out-of-cash-mid-project) and [reasons contractors miss payroll](/reasons-contractors-miss-payroll), see those guides. Sizing the buffer correctly matters: if you run three jobs at once and each has a 30–45 day gap between spend and payment, your total liquidity needs to cover the **peak** gap across all of them, not just one.
