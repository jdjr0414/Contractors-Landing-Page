## Bonding and financial capacity

Many rejections aren’t about your number—they’re about **whether you can perform**. If you can’t get [bonded](/contractor-bonding-and-financing) or your bond capacity is too low, you’re disqualified or not considered. So [why contractor bids get rejected](/why-contractor-bids-get-rejected) often overlaps with [why contractors can’t secure bonding](/why-contractors-cant-secure-bonding) and [why contractors lose projects because of bonding](/why-contractors-lose-projects-bonding). Sureties and owners look at working capital and liquidity. **Fix**: [Contractor bonding and financing](/contractor-bonding-and-financing)—using [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) to strengthen your balance sheet—can increase your bond capacity and make your bid viable. For government work, see [what stops contractors from bidding on government contracts](/what-stops-contractors-bidding-government-contracts).

## Price and cash flow in the bid

You may be **low bid** and still lose if the owner or GC doubts your ability to complete. Or you may be **high** because you’ve built in the cost of [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) gaps and financing, while a competitor underbids and then struggles. **Strategy**: Price with the real cost of [contractor draw schedule](/contractor-draw-schedule-cash-flow) delays and [contractor slow paying clients](/contractor-slow-paying-clients) in mind. Have [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) so you can perform without stretching cash to the breaking point. That lets you bid confidently. For cash flow issues that affect performance, see [reasons your construction company keeps hitting cash crunches](/reasons-construction-company-cash-crunches).

## Qualifications and past performance

Some rejections are about **experience**, references, or compliance—not financing. You can’t fix that with a loan, but you can **avoid** bidding on jobs where you don’t meet the bar and focus on ones where bonding and capacity are the main gates. For those, [contractor bonding and financing](/contractor-bonding-and-financing) and [government contractor financing](/government-contractor-financing) can help. For scaling so you can take bigger work, see [why contractors can’t take on bigger jobs](/why-contractors-cant-take-bigger-jobs) and [what keeps contractors from scaling](/what-keeps-contractors-from-scaling).

## What to do after repeated rejections

**Ask why.** When possible, get feedback on whether it was price, bonding, capacity, or something else. **Bonding and capacity**: Build [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) and [contractor bonding and financing](/contractor-bonding-and-financing) so you can get bonded and show you can fund the job. **Price**: Ensure your pricing reflects [contractor cash flow](/contractor-cash-flow-guide) reality so you don’t underbid. ## How to present financial capacity in your bid

When owners or GCs evaluate bidders, they often ask for **financial statements**, **proof of bonding capacity**, or a **letter from your surety**. They want to know you can fund the job without defaulting. Having [contractor working capital](/contractor-working-capital) or a [contractor line of credit](/contractor-line-of-credit) in place—and being able to show that you have access to liquidity—can strengthen your bid package. It doesn't replace bonding, but it supports the story that you can perform. If you've been [losing projects because of bonding](/why-contractors-lose-projects-bonding), improving your balance sheet with [contractor bonding and financing](/contractor-bonding-and-financing) can both increase your bond limit and make your bid more credible.
